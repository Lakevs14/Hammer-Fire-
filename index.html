01 — Introduction

Why did this fire matter?

Wildfires represent one of the most rapidly escalating natural hazards in the western United States, with climate-driven conditions increasingly enabling faster ignition, broader spread, and more severe burn impacts. Colorado in particular has experienced a dramatic rise in wildfire frequency and intensity.

On April 22, 2026, the Hammer Fire ignited in El Paso County southeast of Colorado Springs near the unincorporated community of Hanover. Driven by high winds, low humidity, and critically dry vegetation, the fire grew to nearly 5,000 acres within 24 hours before being fully contained on April 23, 2026.

Remote sensing provides a scalable and repeatable alternative to costly ground-based surveys. Multispectral satellite imagery captures changes in surface reflectance caused by combustion and vegetation loss, enabling systematic detection and classification of burned areas at scale.

Research Question

How much area was burned by the Hammer Fire, and what was the spatial distribution of burn severity across the affected landscape?

02 — Background

Prior work & key concepts

Remote sensing has become the primary tool for wildfire burn severity assessment. The foundational methodology — NBR and dNBR — was established by Key & Benson (2006) and operationalized nationally through the MTBS program. The transition from Landsat to Sentinel-2 for burn severity mapping has been well supported in recent literature, with 10-meter resolution offering significant advantages for smaller fire events.

📡

Normalized Burn Ratio

NBR exploits the contrast between NIR (high in healthy vegetation) and SWIR (high in burned ground) reflectance to detect fire-induced surface change.

Key & Benson, 2006 — FIREMON
🛰️

MTBS Program

The Monitoring Trends in Burn Severity program operationalized dNBR nationally, mapping all large fires across the US using consistent Landsat-based protocols since 1984.

Eidenshink et al., 2007 — Fire Ecology
🌍

Sentinel-2 for Fire Mapping

Recent studies confirm Sentinel-2's COPERNICUS/S2_SR_HARMONIZED collection is well-suited for multi-temporal burn area analysis, offering 10m resolution vs Landsat's 30m.

Nistor et al., 2025 — Remote Sensing

03 — Data

Imagery & sources

All imagery was accessed through Google Earth Engine. Sentinel-2 Level-2A Surface Reflectance data was selected for its 10-meter spatial resolution, five-day revisit frequency, and free public availability. Cloud masking was applied using the QA60 bitmask, and median compositing minimized residual atmospheric noise.

04 — Methods

Processing workflow

NBR = (B8 − B12) / (B8 + B12) Normalized Burn Ratio — Sentinel-2 Band 8 (NIR) & Band 12 (SWIR)
dNBR = NBRpre − NBRpost Differenced NBR — positive values indicate burned area
01

Define Region of Interest

A 35 × 22 km bounding polygon was defined around Hanover, El Paso County (38.45°N–38.65°N, 104.30°W–104.65°W) to fully contain the fire perimeter.

02

Cloud Masking & Compositing

QA60 bitmask applied to remove cloud (bit 10) and cirrus (bit 11) pixels. Pixel values scaled to reflectance [0–1]. Median composites generated for each temporal window.

def mask_s2_clouds(image): qa = image.select('QA60') cloud_bit = 1 << 10 cirrus_bit = 1 << 11 mask = qa.bitwiseAnd(cloud_bit).eq(0).And( qa.bitwiseAnd(cirrus_bit).eq(0)) return image.updateMask(mask).divide(10000)
03

NBR & dNBR Computation

NBR computed for pre- and post-fire composites using B8 (NIR) and B12 (SWIR). dNBR calculated by subtracting post-fire NBR from pre-fire NBR.

nbr_pre = img_pre.normalizedDifference(['B8', 'B12']) nbr_post = img_post.normalizedDifference(['B8', 'B12']) dnbr = nbr_pre.subtract(nbr_post).rename('dNBR')
04

Severity Classification

dNBR classified into discrete USGS severity classes using .where() chains in GEE. Binary burn mask applied at dNBR ≥ 0.10 threshold.

dnbr_classified = ( dnbr.where(dnbr.lt(0.10), 0) # unburned .where(dnbr.gte(0.10).And(dnbr.lt(0.27)), 1) # low .where(dnbr.gte(0.27).And(dnbr.lt(0.44)), 2) # moderate .where(dnbr.gte(0.44).And(dnbr.lt(0.66)), 3) # high .where(dnbr.gte(0.66), 4) # very high )
05

Area Calculation & Export

Burned area quantified using GEE's pixelArea() function. Four GeoTIFF outputs exported to Google Drive at 10m resolution. Four-panel PNG map generated via matplotlib.

05 — Results

What the satellite found

Burned Area Extent

dNBR analysis identified a total burned area of approximately 20.07 km² (4,958 acres), consistent with official containment reports. The burn perimeter was concentrated in a contiguous patch southeast of Fountain, CO, centered on the Hanover Road corridor.

Severity Distribution

The classified dNBR map reveals a spatially heterogeneous burn pattern. The majority of burned area fell within low to moderate severity classes, characteristic of wind-driven shortgrass prairie fires. Localized high-severity pockets were detected near the origin point.

06 — Conclusions

Findings & future work

It is worth noting that the dNBR classification misidentified several water body pixels as burned area — a known limitation of the NBR index, as water surfaces exhibit low NIR and high SWIR reflectance, mimicking burned ground and producing artificially elevated dNBR values.

This project successfully demonstrated the application of multispectral remote sensing to map the burn extent and severity of the 2026 Hammer Fire. Using Sentinel-2 imagery processed through Google Earth Engine, the dNBR methodology accurately delineated approximately 20.07 km² of burned area, consistent with the official containment report of 4,958 acres. The classified severity map revealed a predominantly low-to-moderate burn pattern characteristic of wind-driven shortgrass prairie fires, confirming that freely available 10-meter Sentinel-2 imagery provides a rapid, reproducible, and cost-effective approach for post-fire damage assessment.

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. The dNBR thresholds were not calibrated against field-collected CBI measurements, and water body misclassification introduces minor area overestimation. Future work could apply an NDWI-based water mask prior to classification, incorporate post-fire NDVI time series to monitor vegetation recovery, or integrate machine learning classifiers to improve discrimination between low-severity burned pixels and spectrally similar dry grassland.

Limitation

No field CBI calibration — thresholds applied directly from Key & Benson (2006) without site-level validation.

Limitation

Water body misclassification produces minor burned area overestimation due to similar spectral signatures.

Future Work

Apply NDWI water mask prior to dNBR classification to remove false positives.

Future Work

Future Work

ML classifiers (Random Forest, U-Net) to improve low-severity burn discrimination in semi-arid grassland.
